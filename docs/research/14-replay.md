# Research note 14 — History, replay & trace depth: the record as the interface

Review slice S-G (SPEC-26) · per ASSAY-DEC-11 · 2026-07-16 · exploratory (decides the approach; does not implement)
Prompts (review `docs/reviews/2026-07-14-jipoe-c2-process-review.md` §6.2, mockups M5/M7, addendum §10 slice S-G): two viewport moves over machinery ASSAY already holds. **(a)** a **decision-history scrubber** — the delta log (SPEC-05) and stamp lineage already form an event-sourced record of the session; a cursor over it replays compiles, refusals, contests/resolutions and verdict flips, the glow (DEC-34) re-firing at each transition — the after-action-review instrument. **(b)** **recursive-on-demand trace tooltips** — the one-hop informs/influenced-by menu (SPEC-16, `traceView.ts`) made recursively expandable to a stated depth cap, the shallow end of issue #24's full dependency-graph view (research note 13). This note decides the four gate questions the spec names: the state-at-seq reconstruction rule (with a checkpointing rule if cost grows), what a replayed refusal renders as, the narrative-runner unification, and the tooltip depth cap. Per DEC-5 the slice is a projection over the existing substrate plus these rendering rules — no new engine (DEC-10), no schema change, no seam change (the delta log and trace graph already cross the seam).

## 1. The doctrinal and prior-art frame — AAR practice and event-sourcing honesty

An after-action review is not a highlight reel; doctrine fixes its shape as four questions asked against the record: *what was planned, what actually happened, why did it happen, and what to do next* (TC 25-20; FM 7-0). ASSAY already holds each answer as a typed object, and the review's §6.2 mapping is exact:

| AAR question (doctrine) | ASSAY object, already built |
|---|---|
| what was planned | the plan + its commitments (DEC-20, SPEC-07/08) |
| what actually happened | the delta log — the ordered verdict/contest/resolution history (SPEC-05, DEC-5) |
| why did it happen | the trace walk from any verdict to its named knowledge (SPEC-02/16, `TraceStore.walk`) |
| what to do next | the sensitivity / discrimination rankings (SPEC-11/12/23, theses D/E) |

The one genuinely new thing is the **rendering rule** that lets an observer *move through* the "what happened" axis without the record quietly becoming a story. That is where a scrubber most wants to cheat — tweening between recorded states, synthesising a "meanwhile", resurrecting a wall clock (FIND-4) — and so, per DEC-11, this note precedes any pixel.

The honest engineering precedent is **event sourcing**: current state is not stored and edited, it is the left-fold of an append-only event log, and any past state is the fold truncated at that event (Fowler 2005; Young 2010; Kleppmann 2017 ch. 11). ASSAY is already event-sourced by construction — DEC-5 makes every cross-surface write exactly one stamped delta, DEC-21 makes objects immutable and content-addressed, so the log *is* the truth and state is derived. This note's job is to say the obvious thing precisely and defend it against the three temptations above. The distinction from **deterministic record-and-replay** debugging (O'Callahan et al. 2017) is the honesty gain: those systems re-execute to reproduce a bug; ASSAY does not re-simulate an operation, it re-derives a belief-state that was actually recorded — G1 makes the two provably identical and makes the replay testable rather than merely plausible.

**Two scrub axes, orthogonal, composed — stated up front.** SPEC-19's timeline scrubs the **scenario clock** (world time, D+n): pure selection of a timestep over a fixed compiled world, no recompute, no glow (note 10 §2). This slice scrubs the **delta sequence** (decision time): *what did we believe, and when did we believe it* — reconstruction, with glow re-firing. The axes are independent — at any decision-time cursor `n` the reconstructed world can still be world-time-scrubbed to select its timestep — and v1 keeps them as two separate controls (a decision-time cursor and, within it, SPEC-19's clock), never a single conflated slider. "When did we believe it" and "when in the operation" are different questions and the surface must not let one read as the other.

## 2. The state-at-seq reconstruction rule, decided

**The view at cursor `n` is defined as the state a fresh store reaches by applying deltas `1…n`, and nothing else.** This is the event-sourcing fold, adopted verbatim: `state(n) = fold(∅, deltas[1..n])`. It is honest because it is *reconstruction, not narration* — every reachable value is one the record actually produced — and it is testable because G1 makes the fold deterministic: the note's exit is byte-equality (§7).

**Implementation is a filter, not a rollback.** Because objects and trace edges are immutable and append-only (DEC-21), nothing is ever mutated or removed, so the fold-at-`n` need not be replayed from scratch: each stored object and each trace edge is indexed by the `seq` of the delta that introduced it (an app-layer creation-seq index, **derived from the log, never authoritative over it** — no schema slot, DEC-5/FR-008), and `state(n)` is the immutable store viewed through the predicate `created_seq ≤ n`, re-driven through the same `snapshot()` pipeline. Filter-then-`snapshot` equals fresh-fold-then-`snapshot` precisely because immutability makes the store append-only in seq order; the two are the same set of objects fed to the same pure services (G1). No "undo", no reverse-mutation, no rollback — those are the mechanisms event sourcing exists to avoid, and they are exactly where a hidden intermediate state would live.

**Refusals and derived surfaces are not stored — they re-derive.** A refusal banner, a staleness flag, a stamp value, a verdict chip is a *function of* `state(n)`, computed by the same gate/service the live head runs (compile refuses `contested_knowledge`, `staleness.ts` walks the trace, the scorer stamps). None of these is a replay artefact to store and re-play; each falls out of re-running the pipeline over the filtered store. This is the single most important consequence of adopting the fold: the scrubber has almost no bespoke rendering surface of its own — it moves the cursor and lets the existing pure components render, exactly as they do live.

**Checkpointing, decided as a rule but not needed in v1.** At the frozen Meridian scale the whole history is a few dozen deltas and `snapshot()` re-drives the pipeline in milliseconds, so v1 re-derives per cursor position and stores no checkpoint. *If* reconstruction cost ever grows (a large KB, a long session), the rule is event-sourcing's **snapshot** (Fowler): cache a `snapshot()` result at chosen seqs (natural boundary: each compile), each cache entry **fully re-derivable from the log and freely discardable** — a checkpoint is an optimisation, never a source of truth, and a checkpoint that disagrees with a fresh fold at its seq is a bug G1 makes detectable. The log remains the only authority; a checkpoint may never be the reason a value renders. Stated so the rule is decided in advance and cannot be reached for as an authoring shortcut.

## 3. What a replayed refusal renders as, decided

**A refusal replays as the refusal it was — because it is part of the record, not an error to elide.** At the seq where the K12 contest breaks, `state(n)` contains the contest and the compile gate refuses `contested_knowledge`; the refusal banner re-renders at that cursor exactly as it rendered live, for the same reason it rendered live (§2 — it is a derived property of the reconstructed state, re-computed by the same gate). Scrubbing forward to the resolution seq clears it; scrubbing back re-shows it. There is no "replay a stored error" path and no eliding one: a refusal is a first-class state of the belief-record, and a scrubber that skipped it would be narrating a cleaner history than the one that happened — the precise dishonesty this slice exists to refuse.

**A refused *attempt* is a recorded position, decided.** The spec's edge case — a seq whose delta is a write that refused, producing no object — is decided as: **the attempt is a delta and the cursor position exists**; the record shows the attempt, the refusal renders, and `state(n)` is unchanged from `state(n-1)` (no object was written). "We tried X and the machinery refused it" is exactly what an AAR must preserve; dropping refused attempts would launder the record. This depends on refused writes publishing a delta (a no-object `refused` envelope). Where the current implementation does not yet publish that delta, the gap is a **walkthrough-§0-class contract defect to log against DEC-5's coverage guarantee** (§7 assumption), not something the scrubber synthesises — absence of a recorded attempt is never backfilled with a plausible one.

## 4. The narrative-runner unification, decided

**Each Stage-7 narrative is re-expressed as a scrub path: an ordered list of `(seq, tab, note)` waypoints over one canonical recorded history.** The runner's job becomes *drive the scrubber* — set the decision-time cursor to the waypoint's seq, raise its tab, show its presenter note — retiring the bespoke beat mechanism (`NarrativeBeat.action`, the imperative `resolve`/`edit-k8` mutations in `shell.ts`) entirely. The honesty gain the review claims is real and is the reason to do it: today a beat can *say* anything in its presenter note while the live state is whatever the last scripted action left; under the unification a beat can only point at a seq, so **a narrative can render only what the record actually contains** — narrative and record can no longer drift.

**One canonical history, curated paths into it.** The five narratives do not each own a private timeline; there is **one recorded heartbeat** — the walkthrough's seq run, extended to cover every act any narrative references (the K12 resolve, the K-band supersede) — and each narrative is a *curated ordering of waypoints* into that single log, in the SPEC-19 sense of a scrub path (a decision-time analogue of that slice's tour beats). A beat that today performs `edit-k8` becomes a waypoint at the seq where that edit landed in the canonical history; the act is scrubbed *to*, never performed live. If a narrative names a state the record does not contain, that is a coverage gap to close in the record (add the act to the canonical heartbeat), never a bespoke action to smuggle back in.

**The SPEC-17 presenter contract is preserved, unchanged.** Beat navigation (prev/next, now over waypoints), the presenter-notes panel, the doctrinal quotes, the centrepiece marker, wall-projection mode, and — non-negotiably — the **banded-honesty presenter-note guard** (`tests/narratives.test.ts`: no narrated midpoint, no verdict-as-score, no weighted-sum language; note 09 §3/§4) all carry over verbatim; the guard now runs against each waypoint's `note` field. The presenter sees the same instrument; the mechanism beneath it is one, not two. This is the register-candidate that touches DEC-23 — the demo-moment machinery — because the runner that *assembles* the banked moments now assembles them *from the record* rather than from a parallel beat list; DEC-23's requirement (each stage exit paired with an observable twin and a scripted demo) is unchanged, only its plumbing.

## 5. The recursive-trace tooltip, decided

**The tooltip is note 13's transitive walk, depth-capped — one traversal semantics, no parallel walker.** The one-hop `traceView.neighbours` reading (SPEC-16) applied depth-by-depth under the `EDGE_ORIENTATION` map is exactly what note 13 decided for the full dependency-graph view; the tooltip is its shallow, in-place expansion. It **must agree with `TraceStore.walk` for the same origin** (FR-007/SC-003): same edges, same directions, same cycle guard — there is no paraphrase layer and no second traversal. Symmetric `contests` edges render on both flanks (once each, cycle-guarded); dead ends render as dead ends **at every depth** (`known: false`, G3) — the reading-flow version of the invariant note 13 lifted to a view.

**Depth cap = 3, a stated constant, not adaptive.** The spec's "~2–3" is decided at **3** and fixed in the rendering: enough to cross the load-bearing chain a reader actually asks for — verdict → world → knowledge object, the three hops from a chip to its named source — without exceeding the ~3–4 chunk working-memory ceiling that makes nested disclosure legible rather than overwhelming (Cowan 2001; Miller 1956; the progressive-disclosure discipline, Nielsen; Crusader Kings 3's depth-capped nested tooltips are the practitioner existence-proof the review cites). The cap is a constant so the surface behaves identically everywhere; an adaptive cap would make the same object decompose to different depths in different places — an inconsistency a reader would (correctly) read as meaning.

**Each hop is labelled from the trace graph itself.** Every expansion renders its **real relationship**: the `edge_type` in the trace graph's own vocabulary (`compiled_into`, `scored_from`, `supersedes`, `contests`, …) and, for the computation edges, a *stated* gloss of the operation that edge records — `scored_from` reads as the interval evaluation of the commitment's metric over the channel reads, `compiled_into` as the band materialisation — drawn from the metric registry's fixed names, never an invented "why". The gloss is a legend over `edge_type`, not a parallel relationship model (which would let the tooltip disagree with `walk`); where an edge carries no computation semantics the hop shows the edge type alone.

**At the cap, an honest remainder — never a silent stop.** Reaching depth 3 with the chain still going renders a stated affordance — "*N more — open full trace*" — that routes to the existing trace drawer and, once issue #24 lands, to note 13's full-screen graph view. This is the G4 no-silent-drop discipline at the tooltip layer (note 13's truncation-honesty rule, inherited): the cap is visible, counted, and escapable. Truncation that hid the remainder would read as "the chain ends here," which for a load-bearing verdict is the most dangerous possible lie.

## 6. What this deliberately does not model, stated

- **Wall-clock time in replay** — none; the cursor orders by `seq` and, within a reconstructed world, by the scenario clock (DEC-17/FIND-4). No recorded `at` envelope is resurrected as a timeline position; the display-only `at` stays display-only.
- **Tweened / synthesised intermediate states** — none; the cursor snaps between recorded seqs. There is no "meanwhile," no interpolation, no motion the record did not contain.
- **A graph layout engine** — the tooltip is depth-capped in-place expansion; the full-screen node-link "river" is issue #24 / note 13's build, kept separate (this slice must not grow it, FR/assumption).
- **Editing the past** — replay is read-only (writes disabled, mode marked); the record is append-only and the head is the only writable point. New live deltas during replay surface as an "M new" count, never a silent jump.
- **A fourth AAR column of recommendations** — the "what next" answer is the *existing* sensitivity/discrimination surfaces reached from the record, not a new advisory panel; ASSAY ranks and derives, it does not recommend (DEC-19, the note-12 discipline).

## 7. What we will do differently

1. **Adopt the fold as the definition of state-at-seq** — `state(n) = fold(∅, deltas[1..n])`, implemented as the immutable store filtered by a derived, non-authoritative `created_seq ≤ n` index and re-driven through the same `snapshot()` pipeline; byte-equal to a fresh replay by G1, with no rollback path anywhere.
2. **Re-derive every surface, store nothing bespoke** — refusals, staleness flags, stamps and verdicts fall out of running the existing pure components over the reconstructed store; the scrubber owns the cursor and almost no rendering of its own.
3. **Replay refusals as refusals and attempts as attempts** — the contest banner re-renders at its seq; a refused write is a recorded cursor position with unchanged state; any missing refused-attempt delta is logged as a DEC-5 coverage defect, never synthesised.
4. **Unify narratives onto scrub paths** — each narrative is `(seq, tab, note)` waypoints into one canonical recorded history; the bespoke beat/action mechanism is deleted in the same change; the SPEC-17 presenter contract, including the banded-honesty note guard, is preserved verbatim.
5. **Ship the recursive tooltip as note 13's walk, depth-capped at a stated 3** — agreeing with `TraceStore.walk`, each hop labelled from `edge_type` (+ a fixed operation gloss for computation edges), dead ends at every depth (G3), symmetric `contests` on both flanks, and an honest counted remainder handing off to the trace drawer / issue #24.
6. **Decide checkpointing in advance, use none in v1** — if cost ever grows, cache `snapshot()` at compile boundaries as freely-discardable, fully-derivable snapshots; the log stays the only authority and a disagreeing checkpoint is a G1-detectable bug.

**Register candidates** (flagged, not asserted — DEC-2; recorded in concept §6): **(1)** the **replay surface** — a decision-history cursor/mode on the observer tab (an `{seq, mode: live|replay}` app-layer state extending DEC-32's shell, DEC-36's shared-surface pattern; writes disabled and mode marked in replay); **(2)** the **recursive-trace affordance** — the SPEC-16 one-hop menu made depth-capped recursive (extends DEC-34's trace-menu companion; sibling to concept §6.29 / issue #24's full graph); **(3)** **narratives-as-scrub-paths** — the runner drives the scrubber and the parallel beat mechanism retires (touches DEC-23's demo-moment machinery: the assembly is now *from the record*, the requirement unchanged). This note asserts no register decision: the fold, the filter, and the refusal/attempt replay rules are *forced* by DEC-5/DEC-21/G1 and FIND-4 once a decision-history scrubber is required at all; the tooltip reuse is note 13's decided traversal under DEC-10; latitude spent (the depth cap fixed at 3, one canonical history, the two-axes-kept-separate v1 posture, checkpointing deferred) is review-slice presentation latitude recorded here, not asserted in a peer document.
